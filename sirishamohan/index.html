As a mathematical biologist, I'm fundamentally attracted to the unique and interesting mathematics that characterize biological systems. More specifically, I'm fascinated by problems that are fundamentally stochastic in nature, often arising in small numbers of interacting agents. Thus my mathematical interests fall under stochastic processes, combinatorics, mathematical physics, and statistical mechanics. This has led me to biological problems in emergent swarms, virus dynamics, chromatin folding, molecular evolution, and the physics of biological assays.

HIV Infection: I'm interested in quantitatively modeling the dynamics of HIV infection and treatment. Specific work includes kinetic models of receptor/coreceptor mediated viral entry, quantifying fusion inhibitor efficacy, and the stochasticity of HIV latency and recovery under anti-viral treatment.

Physics of Biological Assays: Many biological assays can be sensitive to stochastic and combinatoric effects, especially when these protocols involve small numbers of viruses, ligands, receptors, etc. I've created stochastic models of viral infection to inform new analysis techniques for infectivity assays such as the plaque assay, endpoint dilution assay, and luciferase reporter assay. Furthermore, I derived a kinetic model of fluorescent antibody binding in the FACS protocol and developed an automatic gating procedure to increase confidence in flow cytometry data.

Emergence: Biological swarms, molecular evolution (SELEX).

Polymer Physics: X-chromosome inactivation, membrane pore transport.
